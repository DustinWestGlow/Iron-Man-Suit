Let’s represent the human leg as three dots. The thigh, knee, and shin are each one dot in this diagram.
Then let’s bend the leg like we’re walking and look at the diagram now.

Now let’s represent the leg as two rows of three dots. This is because real legs are not flat but have some thickness to them.
Now let’s bend the leg again. This time we have a problem.
We only have 6 atoms to draw a basic leg. So, we are missing atoms when the leg bends and the knee stretches. Our compressed knee expands out into a relaxed structure of our atomic leg. Let’s do it again with atomic pants!
The buttocks stretch when we raise our leg upwards as in a kicking motion. Let's represent the buttocks and leg as a rough approximation using blocks. You can imagine 1x1 legos in this image.
But oh no! When we raise our leg, we move the atoms of our buttocks farther apart from each other. We have a gap in our atomic image!
Let's look at the same problem with flexing our ankle. This chart represents our ankle at a basic level.
Oh no! Flexing our ankle causes the boxes to squeeze into each other.

The problems this page shows is that nonflexible materials like metal in an Iron Man Suit must be fitted together to allow bending and flexing of the limbs of our body. Otherwise, we would be too stiff to move if we designed the suit poorly. This encourages us to design the suit better.
